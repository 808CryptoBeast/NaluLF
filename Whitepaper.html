Section 03 · Core Detection Engine

Benford's Law — Mathematical Fraud Detection

Why Numbers Lie — and How Math Catches Them

Human beings are terrible at generating random numbers. When someone fabricates financial data — transaction amounts, volumes, balances — they distribute their invented values in ways that feel random but leave a statistical fingerprint. Benford's Law is the method that reads that fingerprint.

In 1881, Simon Newcomb observed that pages near the front of logarithm tables wore out faster than pages near the back — meaning lower digits appeared more frequently in real-world calculations. Frank Benford formalized this in 1938. Mark Nigrini brought it into forensic accounting in 1992 and applied it to financial fraud detection. The underlying principle has since been used in auditing Enron, WorldCom, and in testimony that contributed to criminal convictions.

P(d) = log₁₀(1 + 1/d)

Leading digit 1 → 30.1% probability in natural financial data
Leading digit 2 → 17.6%
Leading digit 5 →  7.9%
Leading digit 9 →  4.6%

This distribution is NOT uniform. Fabricated data usually is.

Why It Works on XRPL Transaction Data

Benford's Law applies best to datasets where values span multiple orders of magnitude and arise from organic activity — exactly the conditions present in a real trading wallet. Payment amounts, offer sizes, balance snapshots, fee histories, and token transfers on XRPL naturally follow this pattern when they come from genuine economic behavior. When they come from automated wash trading scripts, fictitious volume inflation, or fabricated market-making activity, the deviation from Benford's distribution becomes measurable.

Real Enforcement-Linked Cases

⚖️
Arizona v. Nelson (1993)
One of the first documented uses of Benford-style digit analysis as forensic testimony in a fraud prosecution. The digit pattern of fabricated payments deviated from expected distribution and contributed to legal action.
📒
Journal Entry Auditing
Major accounting firms use Benford testing as a standard first-pass audit screen for detecting manipulated journal entries, a practice formalized by Nigrini (1999, 2012).
🚢
Trade & Customs Fraud
Economic researchers have applied Benford analysis to international trade data to identify patterns consistent with under-invoicing and tax evasion at border crossings.
🔍
Crypto Volume Analysis
Academic papers have demonstrated Benford deviation as a signal for identifying exchanges with inflated reported volumes — a pattern directly applicable to on-chain DEX activity.

Statistical Thresholds — Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test

K-S StatisticAssessmentInterpretationNaluLF Action
< 0.05Consistent with natural dataDigit pattern broadly matches organic financial behaviorNo flag
0.05 – 0.10Mild deviationCould be normal for smaller or noisier samplesNoted in report
0.10 – 0.20Notable deviationWarrants review alongside transaction contextYellow flag in inspector
> 0.20Strong deviationMaterially inconsistent with many natural transaction datasetsRed flag + risk score penalty
Benford's Law is a smoke detector, not a conviction engine. It identifies anomalous numerical behavior that may justify deeper review. NaluLF always displays the sample size alongside the result and suppresses the signal for wallets with fewer than 50 qualifying transactions. It is one signal in a layered detection model — never a standalone conclusion.

Important Limits of the Method

The law works best on large transaction populations spanning multiple magnitudes from genuine economic activity. It can be misleading on small samples, fixed-price datasets, round-number payments, or values constrained by policy thresholds. NaluLF treats Benford deviation as forensic signal — early detection, not final judgment. Context always matters.

Wash Trading Detection — Five-Signal Composite

Wash trading — executing transactions against yourself or coordinated accounts to inflate apparent volume — is the most common form of market manipulation on DEX platforms. It creates false signals of demand, draws in retail capital, and allows manipulators to exit at the expense of those who believed the activity was real.

NaluLF applies five independent, orthogonal signals to build a composite wash trading score from 0 to 100. Each signal targets a different behavioral fingerprint of non-organic trading. No single signal is sufficient — the composite is what makes the detection robust.

Signal 01
Cancel Ratio
Alert threshold: >55% of offers cancelled without filling
Legitimate market makers cancel selectively when conditions change. Wash trading accounts create offers to show visible book activity, then cancel them without intent to fill. A sustained high cancel-to-fill ratio is a primary behavioral signature.
Signal 02
Round-Trip Counterparties
Alert threshold: any address appearing on both sides
The textbook definition of wash trading is transacting with yourself via controlled accounts. NaluLF identifies addresses that appear as both buyer and seller within the same analyzed period — the clearest possible indicator of self-dealing.
Signal 03
Currency Pair Concentration
Alert threshold: >80% of activity in a single pair
Natural traders diversify across pairs and opportunity. Wash trading accounts focus on a single pair to inflate its specific reported volume. Extreme concentration without diversification is statistically abnormal for genuine market activity.
Signal 04
Fill Rate
Alert threshold: <10% of offers actually fill
Offers placed by wash traders are rarely intended to execute. They exist to create visible order book depth. A fill rate below 10% combined with high offer volume suggests a pattern of phantom liquidity rather than real trading intent.
Signal 05
Burst Activity
Alert threshold: >20 offers in a single ledger close
Organic trading is distributed across time. Coordinated bursts of offers within a single ledger period are characteristic of automated wash trading scripts operating at the speed of the ledger rather than human decision speed.
Composite score > 60 triggers the wash trading warning in the risk banner. A score above 60 does not mean fraud is certain — legitimate reasons include market-maker bots (high cancel ratio is normal for MMs), automated arbitrage strategies, and developer test accounts. The composite score is an input to investigation, not a conclusion.

Composite Scoring Model

WashScore = 0

If cancel_ratio > 0.55 :  WashScore += signal_weight(1) × severity_multiplier
If round_trip detected  :  WashScore += signal_weight(2) × 1.5  // strong signal
If pair_concentration > 0.80 : WashScore += signal_weight(3) × severity_multiplier
If fill_rate < 0.10    :  WashScore += signal_weight(4) × severity_multiplier
If burst_detected      :  WashScore += signal_weight(5) × severity_multiplier

WashScore = min(100, WashScore)
Risk penalty applied if WashScore > 60

8-Module Account Inspector

The inspector is NaluLF's deepest analytical surface. Any XRPL address can be analyzed — not just your own wallets. Each of the eight modules runs independently against the account's on-chain record and contributes findings to the composite risk score. This is the tool a forensic analyst would want if they were trying to understand whether an account had been compromised, manipulated, or used for deceptive purposes.

Module 01 · High Impact
🔐 Security Configuration
Master key status, regular key assignment, signer list analysis, flag checks (lsfDisableMaster, lsfRequireAuth). The first place drain attacks show up.
Module 02 · High Impact
🩸 Drain Risk Analysis
Cross-correlates auth changes with outflow timing. Classifies accounts into four risk levels. Surfaces specific events in the transaction timeline that triggered the classification.
Module 03 · High Impact
📊 Wash Trading Detection
Applies the full 5-signal composite (cancel ratio, round trips, pair concentration, fill rate, burst activity) across DEX offer history. Scores 0–100.
Module 04 · High Impact
🎨 NFT Trap Detection
Identifies free sell offers (price=0), suspicious NFT issuers, and offer configurations that transfer assets when interacted with. One of the most common retail attack vectors.
Module 05
🏦 Issuer Risk Analysis
Evaluates token issuers for global freeze capability, no-ripple flags, transfer fee extraction rates, and issuer account age. Flags tokens that give issuers excessive control.
Module 06
💧 AMM & LP Position
Analyzes AMM pool positions, LP token holdings, voting records, and single-asset exposure. Flags unusual pool fee votes or concentrated positions in thin pools.
Module 07
🔗 Trustline Audit
Reviews all trustlines for freeze status, rippling configuration, negative balances, and issuer address age. Identifies trustlines to issuers with abnormal on-chain histories.
Module 08
📜 Transaction History
200-transaction timeline with type classification, risk coloring, and notable address identification. Applies Benford analysis to transaction amounts. Surfaces burst activity patterns.

Privacy Architecture — Zero Server, Zero Exposure

The security intelligence in NaluLF is only meaningful if the platform protecting it cannot itself become a liability. The architecture is designed so that no single point of external failure can expose user data, because there is no external storage of user data.

🚫
No Identity Server
Names, emails, handles, and profile data never leave the device. There is no NaluLF server to subpoena, hack, or serve a government data request against.
🚫
No Analytics SDKs
Zero tracking pixels, telemetry scripts, session replay tools, or third-party analytics. What you do in NaluLF is known only to you.
🚫
No OAuth Surfaces
No "Sign in with Google/Apple." No third-party auth tokens. No shared credentials that could leak across services.
📡
Minimal Outbound
Only three outbound connections: XRPL validators (public by nature), a price API (unauthenticated GET), and IPFS gateways (NFT hashes only).

GCM provides authenticated encryption (AEAD) — any modification to the ciphertext, even a single bit flip, causes decryption to fail with an authentication error. A fresh 12-byte IV is generated on every save. Reusing an IV with the same key catastrophically breaks GCM security; NaluLF guarantees IV uniqueness regardless of save frequency.

NaluLF connects directly to public XRPL infrastructure over WebSocket, rotating across four public endpoints with exponential-backoff reconnect. This eliminates the need for a proprietary relay server and ensures resilience — if any single node becomes unavailable, the client automatically fails over to the next.

The self-custodied wallet is not the end goal of NaluLF — it is the mechanism that ensures security intelligence and action live in the same tool. Reserve Mechanics

Reserve = BaseReserve + (OwnerCount × OwnerReserve)

BaseReserve  = 10 XRP   (consensus network parameter)
OwnerReserve = 2 XRP    (per owned ledger object)
OwnerCount   = account_info.OwnerCount field

Available Balance = TotalXRP − Reserve
A user with 15 XRP and 4 trustlines has a reserve requirement of 18 XRP — meaning 0 XRP is available to send. This is one of the most common causes of confusion and failed transactions. NaluLF displays the available vs. reserved breakdown prominently on every wallet card and factors reserve thresholds into the bot safety guards.

Objects That Consume Owner Reserve

🔗
Trustlines
2 XRP per IOU token held or issued
📊
Open DEX Offers
2 XRP per open order
🎨
NFTs
2 XRP per NFT minted or held
💧
AMM LP Tokens
2 XRP per pool position

Section 14 · Future Capabilities

AI Agent & Dynamic Trading Framework

The analytics layer NaluLF has built is also a foundation for automation. Security intelligence that detects a drain attack is valuable — an agent that can also initiate remediation in response is more powerful. Portfolio analytics that identifies risk concentration is useful — an AI that explains it in plain English and suggests actions is more accessible.

Architecture Principles

🔐
Local-First Signing
Agents compose and sign transactions in-browser. Private keys never reach the agent runtime, the AI model, or any server. The AI is the advisor; the local agent is the executor; the user is always the authority.
🧱
Sandboxed Workers
Each bot runs in an isolated Web Worker context with explicit, revocable wallet-level permissions. No bot can exceed the access the user granted it.
🛑
Hard Safety Guards
Reserve floor, daily spend cap, and drawdown kill switch enforced at the submission layer. No agent logic can override these — they are enforced below the strategy layer.
