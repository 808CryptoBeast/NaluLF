Section 01

Problem Statement

The dominant model for cryptocurrency wallets involves a trusted third party: a custodial exchange or cloud-connected wallet that holds keys, stores data on remote servers, and acts as an intermediary to the blockchain. This creates four compounding risks.

🔓
Counterparty Risk
Provider can be hacked, go insolvent, freeze accounts, or face regulatory seizure
👁
Privacy Exposure
Transaction history, balances, and identity aggregated by the provider
🚫
Censorship Surface
A centralized service can block users or transactions selectively
⚡
Single Point of Failure
Server outages mean wallet outages — your money, their uptime

For a wallet application specifically these risks are amplified: the data being protected is money. NaluXRP eliminates all four by running entirely inside the user's browser with no server whatsoever.

Section 02

Why Client-Side & Locally Stored

The Web Crypto API Changes the Equation

Modern browsers expose window.crypto.subtle, which provides hardware-accelerated AES-GCM, PBKDF2, crypto.getRandomValues(), and — critically — non-exportable CryptoKey objects. Keys with extractable: false can be used for operations but their raw bytes can never be read, even by JavaScript on the same page.

localStorage is often dismissed as insecure — correctly so for plaintext. But as a store for AES-256-GCM authenticated ciphertext, an attacker who reads it gets bytes computationally indistinguishable from random noise. The attack surface reduces to: know the password AND have physical device access — the same threat model as a hardware wallet PIN.

Why AES-256-GCM?

GCM (Galois/Counter Mode) provides authenticated encryption (AEAD). Unlike AES-CBC which provides only confidentiality, GCM produces a 128-bit authentication tag covering every byte of ciphertext. Any modification — even a single bit flip — causes decryption to fail with an authentication error, preventing malleability attacks and ciphertext forgery.

A fresh 12-byte IV is generated on every single save operation. AES-GCM security is catastrophically broken if the same (key, IV) pair is used twice — reusing IVs allows XOR-cancellation of the keystream. NaluXRP guarantees IV uniqueness regardless of how frequently the vault is written.

The checksum field is verified on every unlock. A mismatch indicates either an incorrect password (decryption produced garbage) or data corruption — in both cases the key is immediately cleared from memory. Watch-only wallets set seed: null and are stored in plaintext localStorage since they contain no secret material.

Section 06

Identity & Multi-Device Model

NaluXRP treats identity as a local profile layer rather than a server-mediated account. Names, email labels, handles, preferences, and encrypted wallet material remain device-resident by default. This keeps the authentication model aligned with self-custody: the application unlocks your vault, but it does not host your identity on a remote service.

Optional backup and future sync features should move only encrypted material. In other words, a second device may receive an encrypted vault blob and metadata, but it should never receive raw keys or plaintext profile data unless the user explicitly decrypts and re-imports locally.

The design goal is portability without custodial drift: convenience features may move ciphertext, but control over decryption remains entirely with the user's password and device context.

Reserve Mechanics

The XRPL requires every account to maintain a minimum XRP balance — the reserve — as an anti-spam measure protecting validators from unbounded state growth.

Reserve = BaseReserve + (OwnerCount × OwnerReserve)

BaseReserve  = 10 XRP
OwnerReserve = 2 XRP
OwnerCount   = account_info.OwnerCount

Available = TotalXRP − Reserve

A user with 15 XRP and 4 trustlines has an 18 XRP reserve requirement — effectively 0 XRP available to spend. NaluXRP surfaces this distinction prominently on every wallet card.

Section 11

Mathematical Fraud Detection — Benford's Law

Theoretical Foundation

Benford's Law describes a pattern found in many naturally occurring numerical datasets: the first digit is not uniformly distributed. In valid economic and financial data, values beginning with 1 appear far more often than values beginning with 9. The formal probability for leading digit d is:

P(d) = log₁₀(1 + 1/d)

This means that in many real datasets, about 30.1% of numbers begin with 1, while only about 4.6% begin with 9. This happens because real values usually grow across scales naturally rather than being spread evenly by human choice. Benford's Law is especially useful when values span multiple orders of magnitude, such as invoices, payments, transaction amounts, journal entries, and account balances.

Layman's Meaning

In simple terms, Benford's Law helps distinguish numbers that look like they came from real activity from numbers that may have been made up by people. Humans are surprisingly bad at inventing “random” financial values. When someone fabricates numbers, they often distribute digits too evenly, repeat comfortable ranges, or avoid patterns they think look suspicious. That can leave a statistical fingerprint.

Benford's Law is best understood as a smoke detector, not a conviction engine. It does not prove fraud. It tells the analyst that a dataset may not look like normal organic economic behavior and deserves closer review.

Why It Works in Forensics

Forensic accountants and auditors use Benford analysis as a triage mechanism. Rather than reviewing every transaction manually, they first test whether a large transaction population follows the digit behavior expected in natural data. If the deviation is large enough, investigators can focus on the subset of entries most likely to contain manipulation, override activity, fabricated invoices, or irregular journal postings.

Real Enforcement-Linked Example

A frequently cited real case is State of Arizona v. Wayne James Nelson (1993). In that matter, suspicious accounting entries connected to an attempted fraud against the State of Arizona were analyzed using Benford-style digital methods. Forensic literature cites the case as an example where Benford-based testimony helped demonstrate that the values did not behave like naturally selected payments, contributing to legal action rather than remaining merely a theoretical accounting exercise.

Plain-English takeaway: if someone tries to “make fake transactions look random,” Benford's Law can sometimes reveal that the numbers are random in the wrong way.

In auditing practice, Benford analysis is used to identify journal-entry irregularities and anomalous accounting populations. In economic research, it has also been applied to trade and customs datasets, where deviations have been associated with suspicious reporting behavior and possible border-tax evasion.

Important Limits

Benford's Law is powerful, but only when applied to the right kind of data. It works best on large transaction populations that span multiple magnitudes and arise from natural economic activity. It can be misleading when used on small samples, fixed-price datasets, assigned identifiers, values constrained by policy thresholds, or highly rounded amounts. For that reason, NaluXRP treats Benford deviation as a forensic signal, not a stand-alone accusation.

NaluXRP should never say “Benford proves fraud.” The correct claim is: Benford identifies anomalous numerical behavior that may justify further investigation.

How NaluXRP Uses It

Within NaluXRP, Benford's Law is one analytic layer in a broader risk model. The platform compares observed leading-digit frequencies against the expected Benford distribution, computes deviation statistics, and combines that result with contextual signals such as wash-trading behavior, cancellation ratios, offer bursts, counterparty repetition, and account-level risk markers. This layered approach reduces false positives and keeps the Benford module in its proper role: early detection, not final judgment.

Section 12

Wash Trading Detection — Five-Signal Composite

The wash trading module scores an account 0–100 across five independent, orthogonal signals:

Signal 01
Cancel Ratio
Threshold: >55% cancelled without filling
Legitimate market makers cancel selectively; wash traders create volume then cancel it
Signal 02
Round-Trip Counterparties
Threshold: any appearance on both sides
Trading with yourself via different controlled accounts — textbook wash trading definition
Signal 03
Currency Pair Concentration
Threshold: >80% in a single pair
Natural traders diversify; wash traders focus on one pair to inflate its specific volume
Signal 04
Fill Rate
Threshold: <10% of offers actually fill
Offers never intended to fill — only to create visible order book activity
Signal 05
Burst Activity
Threshold: >20 offers in a single ledger
Legitimate trading is time-distributed; coordinated bursts suggest automation
Score >60 triggers the wash trading warning in the risk banner. Legitimate reasons for high scores include market maker bots, automated arbitrage, and testing accounts.
